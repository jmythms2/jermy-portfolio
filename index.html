Background

Engineer. Researcher. Simulation builder.

I'm a building performance engineer at the National Renewable Energy Laboratory (NREL), where I develop simulation frameworks that help policymakers, utilities, and building designers understand energy performance at scale.

My work sits at the intersection of whole-building simulation, software engineering, and data visualization. I've contributed C++ to the EnergyPlus simulation engine, built Python tooling for Scout (DOE's stock-level energy modeling tool), and published peer-reviewed research on simulation-driven smart thermostat evaluation.

I believe the most impactful building performance work happens when rigorous simulation meets clear communication — turning 50,000-home parametric sweeps into insights that inform EPA certification criteria.

Energy Modeling · Published Research · SimBuild 2024

Simulation-Driven Smart Thermostat Rating

NREL + LBNL + DOE
SimBuild 2024, Denver CO
52 representative US homes
EnergyPlus · Spawn · BOPTEST · ResStock

Problem

EPA requires 12 months of data from 1,250 installed thermostats before granting the ENERGY STAR® label — making it impossible to evaluate thermostats before they ship. Data-driven methods also can't compare multiple algorithms in the same home under the same conditions.

Approach

  • Built a co-simulation framework connecting EnergyPlus/Spawn (building physics) with BOPTEST (control harness), enabling any thermostat algorithm to be tested against the same modeled home
  • Used ResStock to generate 52 statistically representative US homes across climate zones, HVAC types, and occupancy schedules
  • Evaluated 4 thermostat algorithms: Constant (baseline), Occupancy, Night setback, and Combined
  • Measured both energy savings and occupant discomfort — revealing the trade-off space

Energy Savings by Climate Zone (Interactive)

Hover to explore. Combined algorithm (night + occupancy) consistently outperforms simpler strategies, with significant variation across climate regions.

Key Insight

Generic algorithms save energy via setback — but always at some discomfort cost. The best commercial thermostats move further into the lower-left quadrant (more savings, less discomfort) by anticipating recovery windows. This framework lets us quantify exactly how much better they need to be before EPA certification thresholds are met — without waiting for field deployment.

Data Center Design · Simulation Tooling

Data Center Location & Performance Picker

Data center site selection
EnergyPlus live simulation
PUE · EUI · Grid Carbon
ASHRAE 90.4

Problem

Data center operators selecting a new facility location need to understand how climate, cooling strategy, and local grid carbon intensity interact to determine total energy cost and PUE — before committing to a site. Traditional approaches rely on spreadsheet rules of thumb rather than physics-based simulation.

Approach

  • Built a parametric EnergyPlus model of a representative data center (IT load, raised floor, CRAC/CRAH units)
  • Parameterized by location (TMY3 weather), cooling strategy (air-side economizer, water-side, mechanical), and IT load density
  • Dashboard allows users to select candidate locations and compare PUE, annual EUI, and carbon intensity side by side
  • Results update dynamically as parameters change — simplified version of a full production tool

Key Insight

Climate zone accounts for roughly 30% of PUE variance — but cooling strategy choice matters more than location for most data center types. Air-side economizers in dry climates (Phoenix, Denver) consistently outperform water-side cooling in humid locations (Atlanta, Houston), even when the humid-climate grid is cleaner.
